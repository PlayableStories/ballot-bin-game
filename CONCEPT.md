## 1. Concept overview

**Ballot / Waste** is a short, mobile-first political satire game about how political performance influences the act of voting.

The player stands in a stylised polling station and attempts to cast a vote. Instead of folding the ballot and placing it carefully into a ballot box, they crumple it into a paper ball and throw it towards a container labelled:

> **BALLOT / WASTE**

The player throws by swiping upward on their phone. The speed, length and horizontal angle of the swipe determine the ballot's initial force and direction.

The room is filled with wind generated by politicians' speeches. Each statement reinforces, weakens or redirects the existing wind, altering the trajectory of the ballot.

The central metaphor:

> **The voter casts the ballot, but political performance shapes the atmosphere through which it must travel.**

The game is not about choosing the "correct" candidate. It is about attempting to exercise political agency while reacting to an environment created by competing rhetoric.

---

## 2. Political inspiration

The initial inspiration came from the media framing around the 2026 Clacton by-election.

Nigel Farage resigned his parliamentary seat to trigger a by-election and seek re-election, presenting the vote as an opportunity for his constituents to judge him amid a parliamentary standards controversy. Several major parties announced they would not contest it, raising the possibility that satirical candidate Count Binface could become his most visible challenger.

The by-election is scheduled for **13 August 2026**. As of **14 July 2026**, nominations remain open until 4pm on 17 July, so "Binface versus Farage" is best understood as the election's striking media image rather than necessarily the final ballot.

The contrast is compelling because the two figures represent different forms of political performance — forceful populist certainty on one side, explicit theatricality and parody on the other. This produced the broader theme:

> **When politics already operates as performance, how can the voter distinguish political reality, rhetorical theatre and deliberate satire?**

The game should not permanently depend on Farage or Count Binface. Their encounter is the creative trigger; the finished system should support different elections, candidates and political moments.

> **⏱ REVIEW NOTE — the clock is a live production constraint.** Nominations close 17 July; the election is 13 August. If the game is to have any topical purchase, a playable build needs to exist well before then. This is the argument for proving the core loop with placeholder art *first* and deciding presentation later — see §15 and the build order in `GDD-PROTOTYPE.md`.

## 3. Development from the paper-toss idea

The mechanical reference is the classic mobile format associated with *Paper Toss*: a first-person view, a paper ball at the bottom of the screen, a distant bin, a swipe to throw, a force that pushes the ball off its expected line, and the satisfaction of learning to compensate.

### Original paper-toss structure

An office; a waste-paper bin; an electric fan; read the wind, adjust the throw; land the paper; play for score.

### Ballot / Waste transformation

* The environment becomes a stylised polling station.
* The paper ball becomes a crumpled ballot.
* The bin becomes an ambiguous ballot container.
* **Politicians' speeches replace the electric fan.**
* Political language becomes a physical force.
* Wind changes cumulatively over time rather than resetting per throw.
* Success and failure become politically ambiguous rather than merely numerical.
* The session ends when the election timer runs out and the ballots are counted.

The game may openly reference the familiar pleasure of throwing paper into a bin. Its originality comes from changing what the wind represents, how it evolves, and what reaching the bin means.

---

## 4. Central metaphor

Three familiar phrases, made literal:

**Casting a vote.** The player literally casts the ballot across the room.

**Political wind.** "Political winds," "shifts in public opinion," politicians "blown off course" — these become visible physical forces.

**Throwing away a vote.** The target is simultaneously a ballot box and a waste bin. A successful throw may mean the ballot was counted, discarded, or both.

This ambiguity remains unresolved. The game does not declare *voting is pointless*. It asks:

> Does the vote reach its destination because of the voter's intention, or because the voter successfully adapted to the forces surrounding it?

> **✎ REVISED — the mechanic makes a sharper argument than "futility."** The physics say something more specific than the original framing admitted: *you can only succeed by aiming at where the wind will carry you, not where you meant to go.* Compensation is not a workaround for the theme; compensation **is** the theme. Every design choice downstream — especially the wind cap in §6 — exists to keep that reading intact rather than collapsing into "nothing you do matters."

## 5. Player viewpoint and interaction

Fixed first-person viewpoint. The environment need not resemble a realistic polling station, but it must give a convincing sense of depth: the ballot begins close to the player, the target sits at the far end of the room, politicians occupy the left and right edges without blocking the throwing lane, and changing perspective, scale and shadow communicate the ball's trajectory.

### Throwing gesture

No slingshot, no rubber band, no drag-back. The player:

1. Touches the crumpled ballot near the bottom of the screen.
2. Swipes upward to throw.
3. Angles the swipe left or right to counter the wind.
4. Releases naturally at the end of the gesture.

The gesture controls **swipe speed** (primary force), **swipe distance** (additional force/height), and **horizontal swipe angle** (left–right direction). After release, the player has no further control.

The interface says **SWIPE UP TO THROW**, never "drag to aim" or "pull back to release."

> **✅ DECIDED — swipe angle only. Tilt is cut.**
>
> The three concept boards all captioned the control as *tilt* — "TILT LEFT OR RIGHT TO FIGHT THE WIND," "TILT TO COUNTER THE WIND" — contradicting this section, which explicitly ruled out gyroscope control. The boards drifted from the brief.
>
> **Swipe-angle wins.** One gesture, one input, one thing to learn. Tilt is a second continuous input competing with the throw for the player's attention, and it makes the game unplayable one-handed on a bus, which is exactly where it should be playable. Gyroscope may be revisited post-prototype; it is not part of the core mechanic.
>
> Board copy must be corrected wherever it is reused.

## 6. Political speech and the wind system

Politicians are fictional caricatures inspired by recognisable rhetorical styles. They are **not** representations of real candidates. This keeps the game reusable across elections, less dependent on one news cycle, safer with quotations, and focused on political *behaviour* rather than individual personalities.

Recurring archetypes include: The Patriot · The Outsider · The Manager · The Reformer · The Firebrand · The Optimist · The Strong Leader · The Sensible Moderate · The Anti-Politician · The Deliberately Absurd Candidate.

Speeches may be *informed* by real political rhetoric, but no invented quotation is ever placed in the mouth of an identifiable real person.

> **✅ DECIDED — archetypes, firmly. This is a hard line, not a preference.**
>
> Two of the three concept boards depicted unmistakable likenesses of real politicians. A satirical game about a live by-election that shows identifiable politicians speaking invented lines is a materially different legal and editorial object from one that doesn't — and it throws away the reusability that makes this more than a four-week joke.
>
> **Prototype cast:** THE STRONG LEADER and THE OUTSIDER. Palette **amber and teal — deliberately not red and blue** (see §12). Full character and speech data in `GDD-PROTOTYPE.md` §6.

### Wind accumulation

Candidates do **not** alternate consistently between left and right. Each statement contributes to the current state of the room. A speech can strengthen the existing wind, weaken it, reverse it, or gust.

```text
current wind
+ new speech influence
- natural wind decay
= updated wind
```

The ballot then experiences:

```text
initial swipe force
+ gravity
+ accumulated political wind
+ temporary gust pattern
= final trajectory
```

> **✎ REVISED — the wind is one signed number, and its cap is derived, not guessed.**
>
> The prototype wind model is a **single signed scalar: lateral acceleration in m/s², positive = pushes right.** No vertical wind, no turbulence, no depth wind. One number, four ways to change it (PUSH / AMPLIFY / DAMPEN / REVERSE), exponential decay with a ~5.8s half-life.
>
> **The cap is 4.5 m/s², and that number is load-bearing.** Over the 1.1s flight, the strongest legal wind drags the ball 2.72m sideways. The strongest available counter-swipe (35°) moves it 3.09m. So the worst wind in the game is *just* beatable — and nothing weaker is ever unbeatable.
>
> This is the mechanical guarantee behind §4. The moment wind exceeds counter-steer, skill stops mattering and the game becomes a coin-flip that says *voting is futile* — a lazier and less true claim than the one we are making. **Never raise the cap without raising the maximum swipe angle.** Derivation in `GDD-PROTOTYPE.md` §5.

### Mid-flight reversal

> **✅ DECIDED — once per session, heavily telegraphed, and never more.**
>
> A wind that can reverse mid-flight at any moment is not satire, it is noise, and the player will stop trying to read the room. The prototype allows exactly **one gust per session**, never in the first 20 seconds, announced 0.8s in advance by a rising whistle, paper lifting off the floor, and the hanging sign spinning.
>
> The player sees it coming and can do nothing. That helplessness is the point — and it only lands *because* it is rare. Once per session it stings. Once per throw it is weather.

## 7. Reading the wind

The player must never depend on a numerical wind meter. Political influence is legible **inside the environment**: leaflets skidding across the floor, ribbons of slogan text crossing the room, bunting leaning, podium signs shaking, discarded ballots piling on the downwind side, a hanging **PUBLIC OPINION** sign rotating with the wind.

The words themselves become the wind:

```text
CHANGE          SECURITY        COMMON SENSE
THE PEOPLE      A NEW PLAN      FREEDOM
FAIRNESS        TAKE BACK       TRUST ME
```

A candidate's statement performs three functions at once:

1. Satirical narrative content.
2. Revelation of the candidate's rhetorical character.
3. Communication of the next change in the physics system.

The player must attend to political performance because it directly affects play.

> **✎ REVISED — the indicators are the fairness contract, not decoration.**
>
> Persistent accumulating wind is the best idea in the design and the easiest to make feel unfair. In a 75-second session the player gets perhaps a dozen throws to build a mental model. If they cannot read the wind from the room, the game reads as random rather than as satire, and the whole argument fails.
>
> Therefore: **wind changes land on a visible beat.** A speech fires, the room reacts over 0.6s (bunting leans, leaflets skid, words fly), *then* the throw happens. Telegraph everything. Every indicator is driven from the same normalised wind value so they can never disagree with each other.
>
> **No numeric wind meter ships. Ever.** If a playtester cannot read the wind, the fix is the indicators — not a number in the corner. This is the make-or-break playtest, and it happens at build step 5, before any real art exists.

## 8. Core loop

**Before the throw** — the timer counts down · the room shows the accumulated wind · a candidate begins speaking · they deliver one short statement · the speech reinforces, weakens or redirects the wind · environmental objects react, giving a readable indication of the new force.

**The throw** — a fresh crumpled ballot appears in hand · the player reads the room · swipes upward, angled left or right · speed and distance set the initial force · the ballot travels · the accumulated wind bends its path.

**Resolution** — the ballot lands in the **BALLOT / WASTE** container or on the floor · the relevant counter increments · the landed ballot **stays visible for the rest of the session** · another candidate may begin · the next throw is prepared immediately.

Repeats until the timer expires. A round takes roughly five to ten seconds.

> **✅ DECIDED — throws and speeches never gate each other.**
>
> The player may throw mid-speech. That is not a loophole; it is the situation the game is about. Session length is **75 seconds** for the prototype, subject to playtest.

---

## 9. Ending and ballot count

When the timer reaches zero: no more ballots are issued; any ballot already in flight is allowed to land and count; the speeches fade; the game counts the result.

The camera pulls up and back to reveal the whole room. Every scattered ballot, trapped slogan and leaning campaign sign remains exactly where it fell — a physical record of the election.

```text
17 votes cast.
6 reached the count.
11 were swept aside.
The wind continues.
```

The label on the container rotates between **BALLOT** and **WASTE**, and settles on neither.

No win state. No lose state. No stars, no grade.

---

## 10. Scoring philosophy

The result is the number of ballots that reach the container before time runs out.

> **✅ DECIDED — bin count is the only score. Everything else is cut.**
>
> The original draft floated secondary scoring: streaks, gust bonuses, speed bonuses, and an **"independent vote" bonus** for landing close to the player's *intended* line rather than the wind-corrected one.
>
> At review I argued the independent vote was the thematic heart of the game and should be promoted. **That was rejected, and I think correctly.** Scoring the player for ignoring the wind sets up a second, competing objective that quietly instructs them to *stop reading the room* — the exact behaviour the game exists to make them do. It would also require an end screen that explains itself, and the ending works precisely because it doesn't.
>
> The theme does not need a counter. It is already in the physics: you get the ballot in the bin by aiming where the wind takes you. The player will feel that with no number attached, and a number would only let them argue with it.
>
> **In the bin. On the floor. That is the whole scoreboard.**

## 11. Visual direction

The polling station need not be realistic, but it must deliver: strong first-person perspective; clear foreground, middle distance and target; visible travel of the paper ball; readable left–right wind; politicians clear of the throwing lane; portrait composition.

### Direction A — Photocopied political collage

Black, off-white and grey. Newspaper textures, halftone photographs, torn edges, photocopy marks, hand-cut typography, animated paper scraps. A layered 2.5D collage; candidates as newspaper cut-outs; wind made of headlines and punctuation.

*Strengths:* strongest link to political print culture; visually distinctive; cheap; supports caricature.
*Risk:* depth and trajectory must be very carefully animated or the scene reads flat.

> **❌ REJECTED.** The concept board confirmed the risk rather than dispelling it — on the board itself, it is genuinely hard to tell where the bin sits in depth or where the ball is in its arc. The one thing this game cannot afford to lose is trajectory legibility. Beautiful, and the wrong tool. Worth revisiting as a style for a *static* piece — a poster, a share card, the title screen.

### Direction B — Cardboard political theatre

A miniature cardboard set: corrugated walls, folded paper characters, visible glue and tape, paper slogan ribbons, warm theatrical lighting, puppet-like movement. Politics-as-performance made literal.

*Strengths:* strong depth and material presence; charming rather than aggressive; easy to extend with new paper characters.
*Risk:* polished papercraft assets may demand more modelling or photography than expected.

> **❌ REJECTED — but it was close, and it is the natural upgrade path.** This is the best *thematic* fit of the three: the polling station literally becomes a little theatre in which candidates perform. It lost on production time against a mid-August window, not on merit. If the prototype proves the loop and the project continues, this is where the art should go.

### Direction C — Stylised low-poly 3D

Simple geometric forms, exaggerated perspective, low-poly characters, strong silhouettes, flat shading, graphic campaign colours, with typography and paper debris layered over the scene.

*Strengths:* clearest ball trajectory; straightforward spatial physics; highly readable on mobile; cheapest to animate.
*Risk:* may resemble a conventional casual mobile game unless the print-and-paper language stays prominent.

> **✅ ADOPTED — 14 July 2026.**
>
> Chosen because it is the only direction that makes the arc unambiguous on a small screen, and because it is the fastest to build against the election clock.
>
> **The risk is real and must be actively managed.** Low-poly polish is the visual language of the free-to-play casual game — the genre this project is satirising. That can be the joke or it can be the failure, and the difference is entirely in execution. The counterweights: no coins, no combo meters, no multipliers, no stars, no "GREAT!" popups. Slogan typography, drifting leaflets and paper debris stay prominent. **The game should look like a casual mobile game right up until you notice what it is doing.**
>
> Implementation is 2D sprites (low-poly renders baked to PNG) over a 2.5D fake-depth room — no runtime 3D. See `GDD-PROTOTYPE.md` §3 and §8.

### Original recommended hybrid

> A simple 3D or pseudo-3D room combined with cardboard or photocopied 2D character cut-outs and typographic wind effects.

> **⏸ DEFERRED.** The instinct was right — room for depth, paper for theatre — and Direction C as specified keeps the door open: the room and the characters are separate sprite layers, so paper-cut candidates could be dropped into the low-poly room later without touching the physics.

## 12. Interface

**Top:** time remaining · ballots in the container · ballots on the floor · pause.
**Centre:** politicians and speech effects · environmental wind · airborne ballot · the distant **BALLOT / WASTE** container.
**Bottom:** the hand and the ballot · first-use instruction only: **SWIPE UP TO THROW**, which disappears once understood.

The interface must **avoid permanently coding ideological position through red-versus-blue.** Candidate colours identify individual campaigns; wind direction stays a physical left–right relationship.

> **⚠️ REVISED — the concept boards broke this rule, and the staging has to change.**
>
> All three boards placed one blue politician on the left and one red politician on the right, in mirror symmetry. That composition hard-codes the wind as a red/blue seesaw and hands the player a reading the game does not intend: *both sides push you around, they're all the same.* That is a lazy point, and it isn't the one we're making, which is that **rhetoric accumulates.**
>
> Two fixes, both now in the spec:
> 1. **Palette:** amber and teal. No red. No blue.
> 2. **Behaviour:** the two candidates may push the wind the *same* direction on consecutive speeches, and often will. They are not a see-saw and must never be legible as one. A player who learns "left speaker = left wind" has learned the wrong game.
>
> No wind meter. No combo counter. No multiplier. No coins. (§10, §11.)

## 13. Sound direction

Sound reinforces a cheap political performance space: microphone pops, low-quality PA speakers, paper rustle, podium knocks, room echo, camera shutters, distant crowd, a mechanical counter when a ballot drops in, a soft paper impact when one hits the floor.

**No voice acting and no text-to-speech in the prototype.** Candidate statements are carried by animated captions with microphone sounds and a processed murmur. The caption is the performance.

> **✎ RETAINED — and one line of this section is a design instruction, not flavour:**
>
> *"The sound of the wind should retain fragments of the speech that created it."*
>
> The wind loop is built from a whispered, unintelligible slogan bed rather than white noise. Volume and stereo pan are driven directly by the wind value, so the room is audibly windy in the direction it is visibly windy. It is the cheapest possible way to make the metaphor land in the ear as well as the eye.

## 14. Technical approach

### Phaser 4.1 + TypeScript + Vite, static deploy

> **✅ ADOPTED.**
>
> The game is a fixed camera and a single arc. Fake depth is entirely sufficient for that, and Phaser gets a shareable link into people's hands fastest — which, against a 13 August election, is the whole ballgame.
>
> Each ballot carries `x`, `y`, `z`, velocities, and the wind. Perspective is a projection: the ball shrinks as it recedes, moves toward the vanishing point, its shadow shrinks and separates from it, and the target's collision region lives in world coordinates. Simple, controllable, and unit-testable — the wind, ballistics and gesture systems are pure functions with no Phaser dependency, so the *"the strongest wind is always beatable"* invariant in §6 can be **proved by a test rather than trusted by feel.**

### Three.js

Suited to genuine 3D perspective, cameras, geometry and lighting.

> **❌ REJECTED.** Buys accurate perspective the game does not need, and charges for it in game-state management, collision handling, gesture interpretation and UI, all of which would have to be built by hand.

### Unity Web

Straightforward 3D trajectories, collisions, particles and animation; the strongest option if the bin rim needs true physical collision, or if the prototype might become an installed app.

> **❌ REJECTED for the prototype.** Wrong download size for a link you send to someone on a phone. Reconsider only if this becomes a real product rather than a playable story.

### Speech data — separated from code

Candidate content lives in `speeches.json`, never in the game logic:

```text
candidate · archetype · display text · source status
wind effect · direction · magnitude · slogan words · audio treatment
```

> **✅ RETAINED AND STRENGTHENED — this is the reusability promise from §2 made concrete.** A new election pack is a data swap, not a rewrite. Slogan-word particles are generated as bitmap text from the data, so a new set of candidates needs **zero new art**.

## 15. Minimum viable prototype

1. One fixed first-person room.
2. One **BALLOT / WASTE** container.
3. Upward swipe throw with horizontal angle control.
4. Gravity and wind.
5. Two fictional candidate caricatures.
6. Six to ten short statements.
7. A persistent wind value that speeches modify.
8. Environmental wind indicators.
9. A timer.
10. In-bin and on-floor counters.
11. A final count screen.

**Not needed:** realistic art · real likenesses · voice acting · election simulation · candidate selection · party policies · multiple levels · multiplayer · paper deformation.

The first test answers one question:

> **Does hearing a political statement, seeing it alter the wind and then compensating through a ballot throw communicate the idea that political speech shapes the environment of voting?**

> **✎ REVISED — build in the order that tests the question soonest.**
>
> Grey-box the projection, the shadow and the swipe with coloured rectangles, and check on a real phone that the arc reads as depth. **Stop there before doing anything else** — if the ball doesn't read as travelling into the room, nothing downstream can save it.
>
> Then: catch test and counters → wind as a bare number → speeches and captions → environmental indicators (delete the number) → count screen → art → audio.
>
> **Playtest the central question at step 5, with placeholder art.** If a player cannot read the wind from the room when the room is grey boxes and text, no amount of low-poly polish will fix it — it will only disguise it long enough to waste a month.

## 16. Settled design decisions

* Inspired by the Binface–Farage situation; reusable for other elections.
* Theme: politics as performance.
* Crumpled ballot thrown at a container labelled **BALLOT / WASTE**.
* First-person, fully stylised, convincing depth.
* **Upward swipe only. Angle counters the wind. No tilt, no gyroscope, no drag-back.**
* Political speeches create the wind; wind persists and accumulates.
* Candidates do not alternate left/right and may push the same way twice.
* Statements reinforce, weaken, reverse or gust.
* **Candidates are fictional archetypes. No real likenesses, names or invented quotations.**
* Session ends on a timer (75s, provisional).
* **Score is ballots in the bin. Nothing else is scored.**
* **Visual direction: stylised low-poly (C), as 2D sprites over a 2.5D room.**
* **Stack: Phaser 4.1 + TypeScript + Vite.**
* **Wind is a single signed scalar, capped so it is always counterable.**
* **No wind meter. No red/blue palette. No mid-flight reversal except one telegraphed gust per session.**
* The meaning of "ballot" versus "waste" stays ambiguous.

## 17. Open questions

Answered at review:

| Question | Answer |
| --- | --- |
| Session length | 75s provisional — playtest it. |
| Wind decay rate | Exponential, ~5.8s half-life. |
| Mid-flight reversal? | One telegraphed gust per session, never in the first 20s. |
| Speech as text, audio or both? | Animated caption + mic pop + murmur. No voice acting, no TTS. |
| One politician at a time? | Yes. Never the same candidate three times running. |
| Mark a candidate before crumpling? | No. Out of scope. |
| Points, or only a final count? | Final count only. |
| Ballots trapped in scenery? | No. They land on the floor and stay there. |
| Random variation per throw? | ±2% power, ±1.5° angle. Tune last. |
| Final result: commentary or interpretation? | Interpretation. No commentary, no grade. |
| Which visual direction? | C — low-poly. |

Still genuinely open, for playtest:

* Is 75s long enough for a ~5.8s wind half-life to become legible?
* **Should `DAMPEN` speeches exist at all?** A moderate who calms the room makes the game *easier* — which may accidentally flatter centrism as the position that lets you vote properly. That might be an interesting thing to say, or an unearned one. Watch what playtesters read into it.
* Is one gust per session too rare to register, or exactly rare enough to sting?
* Does **ON THE FLOOR** belong on the HUD, or is it stronger held back as a reveal on the count screen?
* Does the low-poly direction read as satire, or just as another casual game? (§11.)

> **Ballot / Waste is a first-person mobile game in which players swipe crumpled ballots towards an ambiguous ballot bin while politicians' speeches continuously change the wind inside the polling station.**

## 19. Thematic pitch

> You may cast as many votes as time allows.
> You may choose the force and direction of every throw.
> But you do not control the political wind.
